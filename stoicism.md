# Stoicism

Stoicism is an ancient Greek philosophy founded
around 300 BC by Zeno of Citium. It teaches that
we can flourish by mastering our inner responses
to the world, not by controlling the world itself.

---

## The Central Idea

> "It's not things that disturb us, but our
> judgements about things." — Epictetus

External events are neutral. Suffering or peace
come from how we interpret and react to them.
The goal is to live in agreement with nature
and reason.

---

## The Dichotomy of Control

The single most important Stoic idea:

- **In our control:** thoughts, judgements,
  desires, actions, values.
- **Not in our control:** body, reputation,
  wealth, other people's actions, outcomes.

Focus only on what is in your control.
Accept everything else with equanimity.

---

## Core Principles

### 1. Virtue Is the Highest Good

The four cardinal virtues are:

- **Wisdom** — knowing what is good, bad,
  or indifferent.
- **Courage** — acting rightly despite fear.
- **Justice** — treating others fairly.
- **Temperance** — self-discipline and
  moderation in all things.

Virtue alone is sufficient for a good life.
External goods (wealth, health, fame) are
"preferred indifferents" — nice to have,
but not necessary for happiness.

### 2. Live According to Nature

Human nature is rational and social.
Living well means:

- Using reason in every decision.
- Fulfilling our role in the community.
- Acting for the common good.

### 3. Negative Visualisation (Memento Mori)

Regularly imagine losing what you value —
health, relationships, life itself.

- Reduces fear of loss.
- Builds gratitude for what you have now.
- Prevents taking things for granted.

### 4. The Present Moment (Amor Fati)

- Accept what has happened as necessary.
- Love your fate; do not merely tolerate it.
- Focus action on the present, not regret
  of the past or anxiety about the future.

### 5. Cosmopolitanism

We are all citizens of the world.
Our duty extends beyond family and city
to all of humanity.

## Key Figures

- **Zeno of Citium** — founder.
- **Epictetus** — former slave; taught
  radical internal freedom.
- **Marcus Aurelius** — Roman emperor;
  wrote *Meditations* as a personal journal.
- **Seneca** — statesman and essayist;
  wrote *Letters to Lucilius*.

---

## Daily Practices

- **Morning reflection:** What virtues will
  I practise today?
- **Evening review:** Where did I fall short
  of my principles?
- **Journaling:** Write honestly about
  impressions and reactions (as Marcus did).
- **Voluntary discomfort:** Occasionally
  fast, sleep minimally, or embrace cold —
  to prove you can endure hardship.

---

## One-Line Summary

Control your mind, accept what you cannot
change, act virtuously — that is enough.
